The first computer bug was an actual insect — a moth found trapped in Harvard's Mark II computer in 1947, taped into the logbook by Grace Hopper's team.
The term "algorithm" comes from the name of Persian mathematician Al-Khwarizmi (c. 780 AD), whose works introduced algebra to Europe.
Ada Lovelace wrote the first algorithm intended for a machine in 1843, a century before the first computer was built.
The first email was sent in 1971 by Ray Tomlinson, who also chose the @ symbol to separate username from host.
Python was named after Monty Python's Flying Circus, not the snake. Guido van Rossum wanted a short, unique name.
The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 at CERN as a way to share physics research documents.
The first hard drive (IBM 350 RAMAC, 1956) stored just 5MB of data, weighed over a ton, and cost $3,200/month to lease.
Moore's Law — transistor count doubles every ~2 years — was proposed by Gordon Moore in 1965 and held true for over 50 years.
The first computer virus, Creeper, appeared in 1971 and displayed: "I'm the creeper, catch me if you can!"
UNIX was created at Bell Labs by Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie in 1969, originally to play a space travel game.
The C programming language was developed by Dennis Ritchie at Bell Labs between 1969 and 1973 to rewrite UNIX.
Git was created by Linus Torvalds in just 10 days in April 2005, after a dispute over BitKeeper's licensing.
JavaScript was written by Brendan Eich in just 10 days in May 1995. It was originally called Mocha, then LiveScript.
Claude Shannon's 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" single-handedly founded the field of information theory.
The Apollo 11 guidance computer had 4KB of RAM and a 32KB hard disk — less than a modern calculator.
The first transistor was invented at Bell Labs on December 23, 1947, by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley.
IPv4 has about 4.3 billion addresses. The internet officially ran out of them in 2011. IPv6 offers 340 undecillion addresses.
The term "artificial intelligence" was coined by John McCarthy in 1956 at the Dartmouth Conference.
SQL was invented by Donald Chamberlin and Raymond Boyce at IBM in the early 1970s, inspired by E.F. Codd's relational model.
The first computer mouse was made of wood, built by Douglas Engelbart in 1964. He never received royalties for it.
Linux was started in 1991 by Linus Torvalds as a hobby project while studying at the University of Helsinki.
The RSA encryption algorithm was published in 1977. The math behind it — factoring large primes — is still unbroken today.
Alan Turing's 1936 paper on "computable numbers" described the theoretical Turing Machine before computers existed.
The QWERTY keyboard was designed in 1873 not for speed, but to slow typists down to prevent typewriter jams.
The word "robot" comes from the Czech word "robota" meaning forced labor, first used in Karel Capek's 1921 play R.U.R.
Google's PageRank algorithm was named after co-founder Larry Page, not after the concept of web pages.
Wi-Fi was invented by a CSIRO team led by Dr. John O'Sullivan in Australia in 1992, originally to detect exploding mini black holes.
The term "byte" (8 bits) was coined by Werner Buchholz in 1956 during the design of IBM's STRETCH computer.
Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov in May 1997 — the first computer to beat a world champion under tournament rules.
The first domain name ever registered was Symbolics.com on March 15, 1985. It is still active today.
There are over 700 programming languages in existence. Most are never used in production.
The original Apple Macintosh (1984) had 128KB of RAM and a 9-inch black-and-white display.
Stack Overflow was founded in 2008. Over 50 million developers visit it monthly — almost every developer alive.
The first computer game, Spacewar!, was created in 1962 at MIT on a PDP-1 costing $120,000.
Emoji were first created in 1999 by Shigetaka Kurita for a Japanese mobile carrier. The first set had 176 emojis.
Linus Torvalds also created Git. Two of the most widely used software projects in the world came from the same person.
The TCP/IP protocol was developed by Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn in 1974. Cerf is often called "the father of the internet."
The term "debugging" was popularized by Grace Hopper, though she credited the term to earlier engineering usage.
In 1983, Richard Stallman launched the GNU Project to create a free UNIX-like operating system. The kernel was later filled by Linux.
Amazon started as an online bookstore in Jeff Bezos's garage in 1994. It was originally called Cadabra.
The first 1GB hard drive (IBM 3380, 1980) weighed 550 pounds, took up a refrigerator-sized cabinet, and cost $81,000.
The concept of open source software was formalized by Christine Peterson, who also coined the term "open source" in 1998.
Quantum computers use qubits, which exploit superposition to be 0 and 1 simultaneously — exponentially more powerful for certain problems.
The first iPhone was unveiled by Steve Jobs on January 9, 2007. He described it as "an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator."
ENIAC (1945), the first general-purpose electronic computer, weighed 27 tons, used 18,000 vacuum tubes, and filled an entire room.
The Heartbleed bug (2014) in OpenSSL left an estimated 17% of the internet's secure servers vulnerable for over 2 years undetected.
The Mars rovers run on a variant of VxWorks, a real-time OS. Spirit and Opportunity both experienced software bugs that threatened the missions.
The number of transistors on a modern chip (e.g., Apple M3) exceeds 25 billion — more than 3 transistors for every person on Earth.
Linus Torvalds deliberately made Git distributed so no single server could hold the entire Linux kernel hostage.
The first African to win a Turing Award — computer science's highest honor equivalent to the Nobel Prize — has yet to emerge. The continent's time is coming.
